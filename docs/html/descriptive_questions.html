Researcher Discretion in Descriptive Analysis¶

More than anything else, the goal of descriptive analyses is to extract patterns from otherwise incomprehensably messy data. But intrinsic to this exercise is the practice is choosing what constitutes a pattern (and is thus something worth reporting), and what information can be safely discarded. Descriptive analysis, in other words, always entails setting aside lots of information – indeed, the only way to not set aside information would be to literally just hand your original dataset over to the reader. All summarization for descriptive analysis – be that reporting summary statistics and plotting simple distributions, or running sophisticated clustering algorithms or dimensionality reduction tools – is fundamentally about figuring out what data you can set aside to make your data more comprehensible.

But herein lies the hazard of descriptive analysis: because readers are counting on you, the researcher to present the meaningful patterns in the data and filter out presumably irrelevant variation, it is incumbent upon you to ensure that you are highlighting meaningful patterns, and only setting aside irrelevant information.
